Communication & Collaboration

Oct 1st, 2011

I’ve been reading a book on the history of Bayesian statistics. Separately I’ve also been a fan of Neal Stephenson’s Baroque cycle, which I read a few years ago, and sections of the book on Bayes’ Theorem cover the same time period the Baroque Cycle is set in.

One of the things that really stood out for me while reading these book was the sense of time dilation that people then accepted as a matter of course. Or, in reverse terms, the incredible instant-gratification culture we live with today.

For example, then, huge, world changing discoveries in the sciences might have made barely a ripple due to the inefficiencies of dissemination. This was not only due to limited venues for such dispersal, but even where such venues existed, the topic of the discovery had no sympathetic ear to fall upon, for example, a Royal Society meeting where the one like-minded collaborator was unable to attend due to, say, the difficulties of travel.

A few months ago, a friend of mine showed me a mathematical paper he had written that had been accepted on Arxiv (an online journal that is not mendaciously profit oriented, unlike the zillions of other journals out there). In contrast, Ramanujan went for years without recognition in Western mathematical circles, writing his work down in notebooks, which he then sent en-masse to a mentor in England. What if they’d been lost on the sea-voyage? The possibility of shipwreck certainly existed in 1912. The transmission of information was an exercise fraught with hazard, and the useful end-points for such delivery limited.

Or for another example, the other day I was doing some research into skip-list data-structures and was able to easily access the original papers involved, using tools such as Google Scholar and just plain searching. Even a few years ago, this kind of information was hard to come by.

In some ways though, even today, I feel the value of collaboration is under-appreciated. Partly we have conquered the problem of not having a medium by going over to the other extreme – that of having lots of channels, but they are all now incredibly noisy. The value of collaboration is under-appreciated because of the pain one has to go through to find the right collaborator.

In a book by Gary Taubes, a science writer, he refers to the fact that those on the practising side of medicine are far removed from the latest discoveries on the research side. If a discovery does not have a corporation disseminating that information, it is unlikely to get through to the practitioners, most of whom are working off knowledge gleaned from textbooks read during their college years, decades in the past. (In medical specialities, this is less of a problem.)

The bazaar model has been conspicuously successful in software. In fact, companies have been spun out of various facets of the bazaar model, for example, github. But it has not been applied, afaik, to its full potential in research.

Even as a novice researcher, I find it difficult to keep track of all the information I have gathered, and finding collaborators for my multifarious interests is so difficult, that I barely even try.

Imagine how much the world would benefit if it were easy to organize knowledge and gain collaborators from a personal perspective? Unlike say, google or google scholar, this kind of network would spread outwards, from me as an individual to others in the same field, decentralized instead of centralized.

Why should matching algorithms exist (and by hyped by) dating websites, when they are so much more applicable to researchers with similar interests? An academic who’s listed their research interests is much more likely to be honest and comprehensive than a teenager on a dating website like okcupid – which means the conclusions drawn by the matching algorithm are much more likely to be robust.
